01 — what is this
This is a map of BJJ.

BJJ positions are places on a map. Techniques are routes between them. A curriculum is a set of routes you learn to walk, in order.

This project models that map explicitly. Positions are nodes. Techniques connect them. Every route has a start, a path, a destination, and a plan for what happens when things go wrong.

We call the map the fieldboard. We call the routes roads.

02 — the fieldboard
7 positions. 4 actions. One grid.

Every cell in the grid is a position + an action type. The grid is sparse — not every cell has a road through it. That sparseness is the structure.

Blue positions are defensive (you are on bottom). Red positions are offensive (you are on top or behind).

03 — roads
A road is a path through the fieldboard.

Each road is a numbered sequence of cells. It starts at a position, passes through actions, and ends at a destination. Roads are not single techniques — they are multi-step routes.

S S1 — Closed Guard Hip Bump Sweep to Mount Armbar
1. Closed Guard CTRL → 2. Mount SWP → 3. Mount CTRL → 4. Mount SUB
CGRD.CTRL → MNT.SWP → MNT.CTRL → MNT.SUB

Every road carries four properties:

checkpoints
Reusable concepts the road teaches — posture break, mount stabilization, arm isolation. These transfer to other roads.
failure
What happens when a step fails. safe = return to a known position. recoverable = fall to a weaker but familiar position. risky = end up somewhere dangerous.
duality
Every road teaches both players. The person doing the technique learns attack. The person receiving it learns defense. One road, two lessons.
tier
spine = beginner, linear, safe. branch = intermediate, new chains. network = advanced, decision trees.
04 — curriculum philosophy
We teach roads, not disconnected techniques.

A technique in isolation has no context. A road gives it a before, an after, and a fallback. Students learn where they are, where they're going, and what to do when they get lost.

Control before transition.
Transition before submission.
Safe failure before risky failure.
Familiar positions before unfamiliar ones.

Beginner roads are straight lines — no decision points, no branching. If a sweep fails, the student is still in closed guard. If mount is lost, they fall to half guard, which they already know. Every failure leads somewhere familiar.

Branching and decisions come in Year 2 and 3, after the student has enough positional vocabulary to make choices.

Checkpoints are the reusable building blocks. "Posture break" appears in 8 roads. "Underhook" appears in 5. A student drills posture break once and it pays off across the entire curriculum. The system compounds.
